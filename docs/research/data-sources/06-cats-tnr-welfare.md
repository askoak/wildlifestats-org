# Outdoor Cats — TNR and Welfare Framing Data Sources

**WildlifeStats Research Framework — Module 06**
**Scope:** *Felis catus* only. Native wildcats (bobcat, lynx, cougar, ocelot, jaguarundi, margay, jaguar) are explicitly out of scope.
**Framing note:** This document maps the pro-TNR / cat-welfare evidence base for balanced ingestion. It is a literature review, not an advocacy document. Methodology critiques are included wherever they exist.

## Tier 1: Peer-Reviewed Primary Research

### 1.1 Levy, Gale & Gale (2003) — University of Central Florida, 11-Year Campus TNR Study

| Field | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Full citation** | Levy JK, Gale DW, Gale LA. "Evaluation of the effect of a long-term trap-neuter-return and adoption program on a free-roaming cat population." *Journal of the American Veterinary Medical Association* 222(1): 42–46. 2003. |
| **URL** | https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/12523478/ |
| **Scope** | University of Central Florida campus; 11-year program (1991–2002); n=155 cats enrolled |
| **Access** | JAVMA subscription or institutional access; abstract publicly available on PubMed |
| **License** | Publisher copyright; not open access |
| **Data format** | Individual-level program records, population census counts |
| **Update cadence** | One-time retrospective study |
| **Claims supported** | 66% population decline over 11 years; no new kittens born after the first 4 years; 83% of cats had resided in colonies >6 years, indicating post-TNR longevity comparable to pet cats; TNR combined with adoption is feasible for long-term population reduction |
| **Methodology quality** | Peer-reviewed; single campus site (geographically bounded); n=155 enrolled; no concurrent control colony; 47% of cats removed by adoption (a frequently cited limitation by critics — see below); 11-year longitudinal design is among the longer early TNR studies |
| **Known critiques** | Longcore et al. (2009, ABC Birds) note that 73 of 155 cats (47%) were adopted out during the study, meaning much of the population reduction was attributable to adoption/removal rather than attrition from TNR sterilization alone. The study is geographically bounded (enclosed campus), limiting generalizability to open urban environments |

### 1.2 Spehar & Wolf (2019) — UCF 28-Year Update

| Field | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Full citation** | Spehar DD, Wolf PJ. "Back to School: An Updated Evaluation of the Effectiveness of a Long-Term Trap-Neuter-Return Program on a University's Free-Roaming Cat Population." *Animals* 9(10): 768. 2019. |
| **URL** | https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6826864/ |
| **Scope** | UCF campus; 28-year update (1991–2019); n=204 total cats enrolled |
| **Access** | Open access (MDPI Animals) |
| **License** | CC BY 4.0 |
| **Data format** | Program enrollment records, population census data |
| **Update cadence** | One-time retrospective update |
| **Claims supported** | 85% population decline from 1996 to 2019; 11 of 16 colonies eliminated; kitten percentage dropped from 56% (initial period) to 12% (follow-up period); population declined despite significant enrollment growth at UCF; declines are sustainable over extended periods with ongoing management |
| **Methodology quality** | Peer-reviewed; open access; long time horizon (28 years) is a significant strength; same limitations as original (geographically bounded, adoption component); 45% of enrolled cats ultimately adopted |
| **Known critiques** | Same adoption-confounding critique applies. The geographically bounded, managed campus setting may not reflect open urban/suburban environments where immigration of un-owned cats is uncontrolled |

### 1.3 Kreisler, Cornell & Levy (2019) — Key Largo ORCAT Program, 23-Year Study

| Field | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Full citation** | Kreisler RE, Cornell HN, Levy JK. "Decrease in Population and Increase in Welfare of Community Cats in a Twenty-Three Year Trap-Neuter-Return Program in Key Largo, FL: The ORCAT Program." *Frontiers in Veterinary Science* 6: 7. 2019. |
| **URL** | https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/veterinary-science/articles/10.3389/fvets.2019.00007/full |
| **Scope** | Ocean Reef community, Key Largo, FL (~2,500 acres, geographically isolated peninsula with single gated access road); 23-year program (1995–2018); 2,529 cats, 3,487 clinic visits |
| **Access** | Open access (Frontiers) |
| **License** | CC BY 4.0 |
| **Data format** | Individual medical records; aggregate census data (1999–2013); retrospective analysis |
| **Update cadence** | One-time retrospective study |
| **Claims supported** | 55% population decline (455 cats in 1999 → 206 in 2013, p<0.0001); 80% decline in new first visits per year; mean age of active population increased from 16.6 months (1995) to 43.8 months (2017), indicating an aging, non-reproducing population; FIV/FeLV prevalence decreased 0.32%/year (p=0.001); 1,691 surgical sterilizations performed; program demonstrated welfare improvement alongside population reduction |
| **Methodology quality** | Peer-reviewed; longest continuous TNR dataset with individual animal records at time of publication; geographic isolation addresses immigration confound that plagues other studies; retrospective; census counts conducted by single caretaker (potential undercount); estimated rather than measured ages; data collected for programmatic, not epidemiological, purposes |
| **Known critiques** | The study site is exceptional: gated, peninsula, surrounded by water and protected land. Immigration near-zero by design. Critics note this setting is non-representative of mainland urban TNR contexts. As authors note, cat welfare measures are limited to retrovirus prevalence and estimated lifespan; other welfare indicators not systematically captured |

### 1.4 Levy, Isaza & Scott (2014) — High-Impact Targeted TNR and Shelter Intake

| Field | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Full citation** | Levy JK, Isaza NM, Scott KC. "Effect of high-impact targeted trap-neuter-return and adoption of community cats on cat intake to a shelter." *The Veterinary Journal* 201(3): 269–274. 2014. |
| **URL** | https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/24980808/ |
| **Scope** | Alachua County, FL; 2-year program; 2,366 cats neutered (~54% of estimated community cat population in target 11.9 km² zip code); compared to rest of county |
| **Access** | ScienceDirect subscription; abstract on PubMed |
| **License** | Publisher copyright |
| **Data format** | Shelter intake records, field program records, population estimates |
| **Update cadence** | One-time study |
| **Claims supported** | 66% decrease in shelter cat impoundment from target area vs. 12% decrease in non-target area; per capita shelter euthanasia 17.5× higher in non-target area; demonstrates that high-intensity targeted TNR (60 cats/1,000 residents/year) significantly reduces shelter intake relative to lower-intensity areas |
| **Methodology quality** | Peer-reviewed; quasi-experimental design using geographic comparison; large n (2,366 cats); meaningful shelter outcome data; does not isolate TNR effect from adoption (socialized cats adopted as part of program); non-randomized |
| **Known critiques** | Comparison to rest-of-county is not a randomized control; other confounders (enforcement, public behavior, adoption programs) not controlled. Critics note adoption component is integral to the results |

### 1.5 Natoli et al. (2006) — Rome, Italy, 10-Year TNR Study

| Field | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Full citation** | Natoli E, Maragliano L, Cariola G, et al. "Management of feral domestic cats in the urban environment of Rome (Italy)." *Preventive Veterinary Medicine* 77(3–4): 180–185. 2006. |
| **URL** | https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/17034887/ |
| **Scope** | Rome, Italy; 103 surveyed colonies; 10-year TNR program operating under Italian national law since 1991; ~8,000 cats neutered by public veterinary services |
| **Access** | ScienceDirect subscription; abstract on PubMed |
| **License** | Publisher copyright |
| **Data format** | Colony census counts; veterinary service records; city-wide registration data |
| **Update cadence** | One-time retrospective study |
| **Claims supported** | Significant overall colony size decrease (1,655 → 1,293 cats, p<0.001); colonies neutered for progressively longer periods showed greater declines: −16% at 3 years, −29% at 4 years, −28% at 5 years, −32% at 6 years (Spearman correlation = −0.90); program operated under unique legally mandated no-kill framework; large-scale (103 colonies, city-wide data) |
| **Methodology quality** | Peer-reviewed; the largest multi-colony urban TNR study published at the time; 10+ year data; Italy's legal framework provides unusual ecological validity; the study itself notes limitations: 34% of cats at survey were non-neutered, abandonment ("immigration") counteracted program gains in 28 of 103 colonies, authors conclude "TNR programs alone are not sufficient" without complementary education to reduce abandonment |
| **Known critiques** | Authors explicitly state the decrease was smaller than hoped. 28% of colonies increased in size. The study is frequently cited by both sides: TNR proponents highlight the statistically significant population decrease; critics highlight that immigration/abandonment substantially undermined outcomes |

### 1.6 Spehar & Wolf (2017) — Newburyport, Massachusetts Case Study

| Field | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Full citation** | Spehar DD, Wolf PJ. "An Examination of an Iconic Trap-Neuter-Return Program: The Newburyport, Massachusetts Case Study." *Animals* 7(11): 81. 2017. |
| **URL** | https://www.wellbeingintlstudiesrepository.org/aw_comp_globalcats_managementtnr/3/ |
| **Scope** | Newburyport, MA waterfront; 17-year program (1992–2009); ~300 cats at peak |
| **Access** | Open access (MDPI Animals) |
| **License** | CC BY 4.0 |
| **Data format** | Qualitative narrative analysis; historical records, stakeholder testimony, extant program documents |
| **Update cadence** | One-time retrospective study |
| **Claims supported** | Elimination of an estimated 300-cat feral population over 17 years; last known cat died at age 16, 17 years after program began; no cats reported on waterfront since; pairing TNR with aggressive adoption for sociable cats was key innovation; colony was "essentially unmanaged" before program |
| **Methodology quality** | Peer-reviewed; qualitative/descriptive; small number of surviving primary records; retrospective and reliant on testimony; does not establish causal mechanism (TNR vs. adoption vs. attrition); frequently cited as a landmark case but has significant evidential limitations |
| **Known critiques** | American Bird Conservancy (Sinnott 2019 review) criticizes Spehar and Wolf's Chicago study (see 1.7 below) and by extension their methodology broadly. For Newburyport specifically: the study is primarily narrative, population data are limited, and attribution of population elimination to TNR (vs. adoption-driven removal + no replacement) is not rigorously established |

### 1.7 Spehar & Wolf (2018) — Chicago Neighborhood Citizen Science

| Field | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Full citation** | Spehar DD, Wolf PJ. "A Case Study in Citizen Science: The Effectiveness of a Trap-Neuter-Return Program in a Chicago Neighborhood." *Animals* 8(1): 14. 2018. |
| **URL** | https://www.wellbeingintlstudiesrepository.org/aw_comp_globalcats_managementtnr/1/ |
| **Scope** | Chicago, IL neighborhood; 2007–2016; 195 cats across 20 feeding stations, 1 citizen scientist |
| **Access** | Open access (MDPI Animals) |
| **License** | CC BY 4.0 |
| **Data format** | Citizen scientist observation logs, feeding station records |
| **Update cadence** | One-time study |
| **Claims supported** | Colony populations declined mean 54% from entry, 82% from peak; 8 of 20 colonies eliminated; no kittens born after 2009 |
| **Methodology quality** | Peer-reviewed; citizen science data with limitations; single observer; measurement from "entry" and "peak" rather than a fixed baseline can inflate apparent declines |
| **Known critiques** | Sinnott (2019, American Bird Conservancy) provides a detailed methodological critique: the actual decline from first-year census to final count was approximately 19% (not 54–82%); population "entry" measurements are defined inconsistently; counts do not reliably capture all cats; the 2007 Cook County TNR ordinance simultaneously permitted more humane society adoption of cats (21,000 cats adopted out), potentially accounting for population declines independently of TNR. The critique is substantive and reduces the evidentiary weight of this study |

### 1.9 Nutter, Levine & Stoskopf (2004) — North Carolina, 6 Neutered vs. 3 Intact Colonies

| Field | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Full citation** | Nutter FB, Levine JF, Stoskopf MK. "Reproductive capacity of free-roaming domestic cats and kitten survival rate." *Journal of the American Veterinary Medical Association* 225(9): 1399–1402. 2004. |
| **URL** | https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/15552308/ |
| **Scope** | North Carolina; 6 TNR colonies vs. 3 control colonies; 2-year study |
| **Access** | JAVMA subscription |
| **License** | Publisher copyright |
| **Data format** | Colony census counts, comparative (with/without TNR control groups) |
| **Update cadence** | One-time study |
| **Claims supported** | All 6 TNR colonies decreased in population (mean 36% decrease in 2 years); 3 control colonies increased (mean 47% increase); simulation modeling suggested 75–80% sterilization rate required to achieve long-term colony extinction (mean estimated 12.8 years to extinction) |
| **Methodology quality** | Peer-reviewed; one of the few studies with concurrent non-TNR control colonies; small n (9 colonies); short follow-up (2 years); geographic scope is regional but not nationally representative |
| **Known critiques** | Small sample size (9 colonies). Modeling-based extinction projections are sensitive to immigration assumptions. The 75–80% sterilization threshold as a prerequisite for decline is consistent with mathematical models used by critics (who note this threshold is rarely achieved in practice) |

### 1.11 Boone et al. (2019/2022) — Bioeconomic Analysis of FRC Management (JFMS)

| Field | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Full citation** | Boone JD, Miller PS, Briggs JR, et al. "Guidance for management of free-roaming community cats: a bioeconomic analysis." *Journal of Feline Medicine and Surgery* 2019 (published in journal 2022 per SAGE Journals). |
| **URL** | https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/abs/10.1177/1098612X211055685 (SAGE); open-access version: https://academicworks.cuny.edu/hc_pubs/743/ |
| **Scope** | Computer simulation modeling; open demographic environments; starting population 50 cats; 10-year simulation; cost data from 2,265 shelter/rescue organizations |
| **Access** | Open access preprint at CUNY Academic Works; SAGE subscription for published version |
| **License** | Publisher copyright (journal); preprint accessible |
| **Data format** | Stochastic individual-based simulation model (Vortex software); itemized cost survey data |
| **Update cadence** | One-time modeling study |
| **Claims supported** | At equivalent 45% population reduction outcomes, sterilization-only scenarios were considerably less expensive than removal-only scenarios; TNR is viable and potentially more cost-effective than lethal removal at higher intensities; all methods require sufficient intensity; cat abandonment minimization is critical to all approaches |
| **Methodology quality** | Peer-reviewed; published in a high-quality feline medicine journal; modeling-based conclusions limited by underlying assumptions; cost data from a voluntary survey of sheltering organizations |
| **Known critiques** | Simulation models are sensitive to assumptions about immigration, abandonment rates, and initial population structure. Critics of TNR economic claims note that volunteer labor and donated veterinary services in TNR are often not fully costed |

### 1.14 Gehrt et al. (2013) — Urban Coyotes Suppress Free-Roaming Cat Use of Natural Areas

| Field | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Full citation** | Gehrt SD, Wilson EC, Brown JL, Anchor C. "Population Ecology of Free-Roaming Cats and Interference from Urban Coyotes." *PLOS ONE* 8(9): e75718. 2013. |
| **URL** | https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3772906/ |
| **Scope** | Urban Chicago area; 2-year mark-recapture radio-telemetry study of 39 cats and 36 coyotes; urban parks and greenspaces |
| **Access** | Open access (PLOS ONE) |
| **License** | CC BY 4.0 |
| **Data format** | Radio-telemetry tracking data; mark-recapture; 2-year longitudinal |
| **Update cadence** | One-time study |
| **Claims supported** | Cats avoid core activity areas of coyotes; coyotes "essentially exclude cats from natural habitat fragments"; cats restrict core activities to developed urban zones (near homes and shops); 80% 2-year survival rate — longer than expected, comparable to area coyotes; coyotes serve as de facto ecological buffer limiting cat predation in protected natural areas |
| **Methodology quality** | Peer-reviewed; methodologically rigorous (telemetry, mark-recapture); n is limited; urban Chicago parks may not generalize to all habitat types |
| **Known critiques** | As Gehrt himself notes: low cat impact on urban natural areas does not imply low impact in developed zones where cats concentrate. This study is often cited by TNR proponents to argue cats have limited wildlife impact, but the authors explicitly disclaim that inference for developed zones |

### 1.15 Kravetz (2002) — Yale Review: Toxoplasma Risk Primarily Food-Borne for Pregnant Women

| Field | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Full citation** | Kravetz JD, Federman DG. "Toxoplasmosis in pregnancy." *Archives of Internal Medicine* 162(14): 1585–1587. 2002. |
| **URL** | https://news.yale.edu/2002/09/26/yale-physician-reviews-research-dispel-myth-about-pregnant-women-and-cats |
| **Scope** | Narrative review of toxoplasmosis transmission routes; focus on pregnant women |
| **Access** | Archives of Internal Medicine (subscription); press release publicly available |
| **License** | Publisher copyright |
| **Data format** | Narrative review |
| **Update cadence** | One-time review (2002) |
| **Claims supported** | Pregnant women are at higher risk of acquiring toxoplasmosis from undercooked meat than from the family cat; cats shed oocysts for only ~2 weeks of their lives (typically as kittens); regular litter box cleaning diminishes contact with infective feces; the primary infection risk profile for humans relates to meat consumption and soil contact, not direct cat contact |
| **Methodology quality** | Peer-reviewed review; widely cited; data from 2002; CDC and other authoritative bodies recognize both foodborne and cat-contact routes as significant, so this study represents one end of the evidential spectrum, not consensus |
| **Known critiques** | More recent reviews (Dabritz & Conrad 2010; VanWormer et al. 2016) emphasize that oocyst-mediated environmental transmission is a major underappreciated route, especially in areas with high feral cat density. The food-borne framing is accurate but incomplete — environmental oocyst contamination from outdoor cats (owned and feral) is a genuine public health concern that is not eliminated by TNR |

## Tier 2: NGO Data and Surveillance Programs

### 2.1 University of Florida Maddie's Shelter Medicine Program

| Field | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Name & URL** | UF Maddie's Shelter Medicine Program — https://sheltermedicine.vetmed.ufl.edu |
| **Scope** | Academic research program (Gainesville, FL); houses Operation Catnip; publishes peer-reviewed research; conducts Florida Shelter Animal Census annually since 2013; leads/co-leads Million Cat Challenge |
| **What data they hold** | Individual cat records from Operation Catnip (>90,000 cats sterilized since 1998); Florida Shelter Animal Census (all 153 FL shelters; intake, outcome, euthanasia, RTF data annually); published studies on TNR effectiveness, shelter medicine, feline infectious disease |
| **Access** | Florida Shelter Census reports publicly available online; Operation Catnip aggregate data published; peer-reviewed studies via journals (many open access via Frontiers, PLOS ONE, Animals) |
| **License** | Published data: journal-specific; census data: publicly available summary reports |
| **Data format** | Annual report PDFs; peer-reviewed journal articles; textbook chapters |
| **Update cadence** | Florida Shelter Animal Census: annual; research publications: ongoing |
| **Specific claims supported** | In Alachua County, Operation Catnip's TNR program reduced shelter cat euthanasia from 81% to 42% over 13 years; adding RTF dropped rate to 8% (2018); 2024 Florida census: RTF responsible for 21% of all live outcomes for cats; rural shelters with TNR/RTF programs had higher live outcome rates than those without (2025 study in JSMCAH) |
| **Methodology quality** | Academic; peer-reviewed; Florida Census is census-based (all 153 shelters, 100% coverage in 2024); strong methodological transparency |
| **Known critiques** | Operation Catnip data covers a single metro area (Alachua County) with specific demographic conditions. Julie Levy's multiple roles as researcher, Operation Catnip founder, and Million Cat Challenge co-leader create potential advocacy-research alignment; this is disclosed in publications but relevant to bias assessment |

### 2.2 Shelter Animals Count (SAC) National Database

| Field | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Name & URL** | Shelter Animals Count — https://www.shelteranimalscount.org |
| **Scope** | National (U.S.); aggregates intake and outcome data from ~5,000+ shelters and rescue organizations; tracks cats and dogs; includes TNR/RTF counts via Community Services Matrix |

| **Update cadence** | Monthly data upload (automated via API on ~5th/6th of each month for prior month); annual reports |
| **Specific claims supported** | National shelter cat euthanasia trends (declining); RTF as growing share of live outcomes; year-over-year comparisons of shelter performance |
| **Methodology quality** | Not peer-reviewed; voluntary participation creates potential selection bias (progressive shelters more likely to report); SAC data is the closest thing to a national shelter surveillance system for cats; data quality varies by submission method |
| **Known critiques** | Voluntary participation means results are not nationally representative; organizations reporting to SAC skew toward engaged, typically more progressive shelters; SAC's "live outcome rate" metric does not distinguish between adoption and RTF/SNR in all presentations, which can obscure what "saving" means for community cats |

### 2.3 Best Friends Animal Society — National Shelter Data & Community Cat Programs

| Field | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Name & URL** | Best Friends Animal Society — https://bestfriends.org; Shelter Stats: https://bestfriends.org/no-kill/animal-shelter-statistics |
| **Scope** | National (U.S.); data from 10,000+ shelters and rescue groups; tracks no-kill progress; operates community cat programs including NKLA (Los Angeles); publishes annual National Shelter Data Report |
| **What data they hold** | Annual national kill/save rate statistics; state-by-state breakdowns; community cat program outcomes; TNVR program data; Community Cat Challenge results |
| **Access** | Annual reports publicly available; online dashboard; no API for external researchers |
| **License** | Publicly available; proprietary methodology (Best Friends applies estimation models for non-reporting shelters) |
| **Data format** | Annual PDF reports; online dashboards; press releases |
| **Update cadence** | Annual (2025 data released 2026) |
| **Specific claims supported** | Cat kill rate decreased 72.5% from 2016 to 2024; 2025: record-high cat save rate of 82.9%; number of cats safely returned to outdoor homes increased ~70% over past decade; 2025 data: 34,000 fewer dogs and cats killed vs. 2024 (8.1% drop); 66%+ of U.S. shelters reached no-kill in 2025 |
| **Methodology quality** | Not peer-reviewed; Best Friends uses estimation for ~20% of shelters without current data (peer-reviewed methodology approval claimed); the 90% no-kill threshold is an industry standard but not a regulatory benchmark; attribution of trends to community cat programs vs. other factors is observational |
| **Known critiques** | Best Friends is an advocacy organization; its data is used in its own campaigns. The 90% save rate "no-kill" benchmark is contested — some argue it permits euthanasia of animals that might be saveable. The estimation methodology for non-reporting shelters introduces uncertainty. Critics note that RTF/SNR returning cats to outdoor environments represents a different kind of "save" than adoption |

### 2.4 Million Cat Challenge

| Field | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Name & URL** | Million Cat Challenge — https://www.millioncatchallenge.org; UF: https://sheltermedicine.vetmed.ufl.edu/shelter-services/the-right-outcome/million-cat-challenge/ |

## Cross-Cutting Methodological Notes for WildlifeStats

### On the "colony size reduction" claim
The strongest evidence for colony size reduction under TNR comes from geographically bounded or isolated settings (gated communities, campuses, linear trails). Open urban environments show more variable results — particularly where cat abandonment continues and immigration is uncontrolled. The Natoli et al. (2006) Rome study is the most ecologically realistic large-scale dataset, and its authors themselves concluded that TNR alone is insufficient. WildlifeStats should flag geographic setting as a moderator variable.

### On the "TNR reduces shelter intake and euthanasia" claim
This claim has the strongest real-world evidence base, particularly from the Alachua County Operation Catnip data and the national trends captured by Best Friends/SAC. The mechanism (preventing kittens from entering the system) is biologically plausible and consistently observed. However, shelter-level outcome improvements are confounded by concurrent policy changes, cultural shifts, and adoption program expansions. The Levy et al. (2014) quasi-experimental design is the strongest study on this mechanism.

### On Toxoplasma gondii
The foodborne framing (Kravetz 2002, Yale) is accurate in characterizing meat consumption as a major transmission route, but more recent epidemiology (Dabritz & Conrad 2010; CDC guidance) treats both food-borne and oocyst-mediated environmental transmission as significant. TNR does not reduce T. gondii oocyst environmental loading — neutered cats that continue to hunt and defecate outdoors continue to shed oocysts (for the ~2-week window following first infection). This is a genuine limitation of the pro-TNR T. gondii framing.

### On wildlife predation context
The Gehrt et al. (2013) finding that urban coyotes suppress free-roaming cat use of natural areas is a legitimate empirical finding, but Gehrt himself cautions it does not imply low cat impact in developed zones. TNR proponents citing this study should note that coyotes are not uniformly distributed and this effect is geographically specific to areas with established coyote populations.

### On the "vacuum effect"
The hypothesis that removing cats from an area causes immigration of un-owned cats is cited by TNR proponents to argue against lethal control. Empirical support is mixed: some studies show rebound (consistent with vacuum effect); others show that sustained, high-intensity removal can achieve lasting reductions. The AVMA policy's "goal of attrition" language implicitly accepts this as a valid concern.
